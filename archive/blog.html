The Death of Data Science: A Eulogy for Human Analytics

We are witnessing the end of an era. The field I once called home—data science—is being systematically automated out of existence. Machine learning platforms now generate insights faster than humans can think. AutoML tools build models better than most practitioners. And AI agents are beginning to ask and answer business questions independently.

But perhaps this isn't a tragedy. Perhaps it's evolution. As the last living data scientist, I've had a front-row seat to this transformation, and I'm here to tell you: the death of data science as we know it might be the best thing that ever happened to it.

The Art of Feature Engineering: A Dying Craft

In the old days—which is to say, three years ago—feature engineering was the heart of machine learning. We would spend weeks crafting the perfect features, combining domain knowledge with statistical intuition to create variables that would help our models see patterns in the data.

Now, neural networks learn features automatically, and automated ML platforms handle feature selection without human intervention. But I argue that feature engineering isn't dead—it's just evolved into something more profound: the art of asking the right questions.
